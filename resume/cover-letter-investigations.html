Dear Gabriel Dance,

Some stories can only be told when you find what was meant to stay hidden — and then make it impossible to ignore. When the subject is a system of exploitation, a pattern buried in millions of records, or data that was never meant to see daylight, the right visualization doesn't just illustrate the reporting; it becomes the evidence. That's why I'm applying for the Graphics/Multimedia Editor position on the Investigations desk.

I understand what investigative work demands because I've done it. As a fraud investigator at the U.S. Embassy in Mexico City, I examined visa applications for patterns of deception, built cases from incomplete and contradictory data, and developed training programs to help colleagues recognize what they were seeing. That experience taught me something I carry into every project: investigation is about making the invisible visible — finding the signal in noise, then presenting it so clearly that no one can look away.

That instinct is what drives Planeta Mujer, the interactive data visualization platform I built to surface the hidden realities of gender inequality worldwide. "50 Million," a scroll-driven narrative on modern slavery, renders 10,000 HTML Canvas particles that morph into country shapes through D3 force simulation — contextualizing 50 million victims against real populations and historical atrocities. I built a scrollytelling world map of World Bank gender equality scores across 190 countries, an SVG hourglass comparing gendered time use across 30 OECD nations, and an interactive sunburst taxonomy of feminist thought. Every piece starts with raw data and a question: what is this information hiding, and how do I make it undeniable?

For the past almost five years as a front-end engineer at The New York Times, I've been building interactive, content-driven applications. I collaborate daily with designers, editorial teams, and product developers. I know firsthand how this organization works — its commitment to quality, its culture of accuracy, and the rigor that its best journalism demands. The Investigations desk's work — from the reporting on online dangers to children to the exposure of sensitive location data harvesting — represents exactly the kind of storytelling where engineering, data analysis, and journalism become inseparable. That intersection is where I'd love to work — as part of a team whose journalism makes a real impact on people's lives.

What I'd bring to the Investigations desk is a combination that maps directly to what this work demands: the engineering skills to build interactive visualizations and reporting tools from scratch (D3.js, Canvas, Scrollama, Python, R), the investigative instincts I developed building fraud cases at the Embassy, over 140 published explanatory articles, and a master's-level grounding in social policy. I'm bilingual in English and Spanish — an asset for international investigations — and I'm currently completing NYU's Modern Journalism certificate to deepen my foundations in investigative reporting and multimedia storytelling. I'm deeply committed to the kind of journalism that holds systems accountable by making their data speak.

I would love the opportunity to bring my technical skills, my investigative instincts, and my belief in the power of visual storytelling to the Investigations team.

Sincerely,
Monica Cholico
